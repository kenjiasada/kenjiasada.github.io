There are shortage of "konbini" in western Japan?

Konbini is now an indispensable lifeline for Japanese people. Not only selling foods or daily goods, but it provides a lot of neccesary services for their daily life --- payment of tax, postal service, withdrawing money... Through a location data research, I found that konbinies distribution is skewd to east and north side of Japan

By KENJI ASADA

Published August 3, 2019

Methodology: I scraped "MapFan"(an website which provides commercial location info) to build the list of konbinies. After I build the list, I cleaned that data first, and then input those addresses into Google Geocoding API to get longitude and latitude. I put geolocation data to QGIS and merged that konbini data with munisipality polygon data.

Notes: As for June 30, the number of stores for each konbini brand are: Seven-Eleven 20,973, Family Mart 16,438, LAWSON 14,659(Feb. 28), Mini Stop 2,004. This number is different from my dataset. (Especially the I have 24,000 SEVEN-ELEVEN data...) It maybe because there are still some dupulicates or because some data in MapFan are too old.

In west japan, especially in Kyushu district, it is said that drug stores are prominent. I'm not sure the reason, but I think someone says there is relation with income.

If I plot the drug stores, it probably will be a different scenery... I know that if I can get more accurate data if I scrape the official sites of every convenience stores. But it seems impossible to do that due to the structure of the websites.

Seven-Eleven is prominent in the most of Japan

Seven-Eleven has apparently the top share in Japanese konbini market. But in some part of Japan, other brands shows strongness. For example, Seico Mart focuses on countryside in Hokkaido and Lawson has large share in Okinawa. Historically, Lawson is popular in west Japan, and Family Mart and Ministop has strong competence in central area (around Nagoya).

Image: KENJI ASADA based on info from MapFan

In north & east areas, people demand more konbinies...

Hokkaido and Tohoku look more red than Kansai and Shikoku. The number of konbinies per population is larger in north and east Japan. There seem to be some reasons. Population density, the difference of chain management strategies, competition with other type of shops like drug stores or super market, etc.
